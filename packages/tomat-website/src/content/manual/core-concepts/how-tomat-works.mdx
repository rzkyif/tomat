tomat is built from two programs with a clear division of labor. The **core** is
a service that holds your data and does the heavy lifting: it stores sessions,
runs models, executes tools, and answers requests. The **client** is the desktop
app you see: it draws the conversation, captures what you type, plays audio, and
otherwise stays thin. Understanding that split makes the rest of the manual fall
into place, because most settings belong to one side or the other.

## Core and client

The core runs quietly in the background and owns every stateful and computational
concern. When you send a message, it is the core that streams the model's reply,
ranks which tools to offer, and reads your documents. The client never does that
work itself; it asks the core and renders what comes back.

Because the core is a separate service, it does not have to live where the client
does. The two usually run on the same machine, but a client can pair with a core
on a more powerful desktop and drive it from a laptop. When a session is running
against a named core, the session bar tells you which one.

That separation is also why settings split the way they do: how the app looks is a
**client** setting, while which model answers you is a **core** setting. The
[Settings Panel](/manual/settings/settings-panel) marks each group with the side
it belongs to, and [Cores and Pairing](/manual/settings/cores-and-pairing) covers
running more than one core.

## Local by default

Everything tomat stores lives under `~/.tomat` on the machine running the core:
your sessions, documents, snippets, downloaded models, and the settings you have
changed. Nothing about a conversation leaves that machine unless you point a
setting at an **External** provider, and tomat tells you when you have.

This is the trade you are opting into when you choose a Local model. A Local model
runs on your own hardware, so its speed depends on your machine, but the
conversation never goes anywhere. An External provider can be faster or more
capable, at the cost of sending your messages to it.

<Aside type="note">
  Large model files are the one thing shared across installs rather than copied: they sit in a
  common location so a multi-gigabyte download is fetched once, not per channel. See [Updates and
  Channels](/manual/settings/updates-and-channels).
</Aside>

## What shapes a reply

Once you send a message, three things decide what comes back, and each has its own
chapter:

- The **model** is the engine that writes the reply, and a few dials shape how it
  answers. See [Models and Providers](/manual/core-concepts/models-and-providers)
  and [Thinking and Creativity](/manual/tuning-replies/thinking-and-creativity).
- **Tools** let the agent act beyond writing text, like searching the web or
  running a calculation. See [Tools and Toolkits](/manual/tools/tools-and-toolkits).
- **Knowledge** is what you have given tomat to draw on: your documents and
  snippets. See [Documents](/manual/knowledge/documents).

You see all three at work in the conversation itself. A reply streams in as a
bubble; a Thinking bubble shows the model reasoning; tool runs and consulted
documents appear inline as the agent uses them.
